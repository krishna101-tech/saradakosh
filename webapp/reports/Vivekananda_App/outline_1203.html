England: Fall, 1896 - II

1 7 Jun 1896 S Swamiji in his letter to Margaret Noble on June 7, 1896 writes about his ideal to preach unto mankind. (ND 4. 446)
2 5 Nov 1896 S In his second series Swamiji would take up the Chhandogya Upanishad, starting on Thursday morning, November 5, with `The Freedom of the Soul." (ND 4. 447)
3 Nov 1896 S It would not be long before the outcome of the battle monotheistic religion was waging against science would be decisive. (ND 4. 447)
4 Nov 1896 S Swamiji tried in many ways to show that on its deeper levels the religious way of looking at the universe was not "unscientific." (ND 4. 449)
5 Nov 1896 S Josiah Goodwin wrote glowingly of that Wednesday evening class of November 18. "The Swami's lectures seem to be better and better every time," he told Mrs. Bull. 7 Nov 1896 S Thus Swamiji, reconciling science and Advaita Vedanta by pointing out the fundamental principles of knowledge common to the both, took nothing away; rather, he enhanced everything. (ND 4. 452)
8 Nov 1896 S Swamiji did not give the impression that it was an easy matter to make one's life, whatever its circumstances might be, coincide with the ideal. (ND 4. 453)
9 Nov 1896 S He provided many lines of reasoning by which the student could intellectually comprehend the truth of and necessity for Advaita Vedanta. (ND 4. 454)
10 Nov 1896 S Now in this series on practical Vedanta he did indeed combine them, for though these classes were concerned primarily with jnana yoga, it was a jnana yoga that incorporated into itself an Advaita-based karma yoga as well as a bhakti yoga. (ND 4. 456)
11 Nov 1896 S Thus Swamiji, emphasizing the positive side of Advaita Vedanta, brought it to the marketplaces of the world. (ND 4. 457)
12 Nov 1896 S Simplicity of Swamiji's practical presentation of the most profound philosophy was one of his unique contributions to religious culture. (ND 4. 459)
13 Nov 1896 S Strength, therefore, is the one thing needful [Swamiji declared in "The Freedom of the Soul"]. (ND 4. 461)
14 Nov 1896 S Bold it was. Swamiji used to make bold statements. He knew there would be alarm and resistance, but he went ahead, for he also knew that his listeners had come to him out of a desire for truth and that sooner or later they would make it their own. (ND 4. 463)
15 Nov 1896 S Again and again throughout his classes on practical Vedanta Swamiji urged the service of one's fellowman. Any work, whatever its field might be-art, science, education, law, commerce, anything-could be transformed into service and laid at man's feet, as at the feet of God. (ND 4. 464)
16 Nov 1896 S But what was the difference in effect, as far as ethics was concerned, between the service of man based on the monistic ideal and that based on brotherly love as taught by theistic religions? (ND 4. 464)
17 Nov 1896 S The other side of exclusiveness was fear. If God has favorites, saving some and not others, then it well behooves His devotees to stay in good favor. (ND 4. 466)
18 Nov 1896 S Swamiji's analysis of the failings of dualism was a prod, as it were, to his listeners; he urged them to break through all confining beliefs. (ND 4. 467)
19 Nov 1896 S Swamiji cautioned his class at the close of "Practical Vedanta-II" (for he had been urging his listeners to move forward) (ND 4. 467)
20 Nov 1896 S Swamiji said in "The Freedom of the Soul" that he had no objection to dualism in many of its forms. (ND 4. 468)
21 Nov 1896 S Swamiji never inveighed against devotion to the Personal God; on the contrary, he always spoke of true devotion to God in any form or aspect as a sure path to freedom. (ND 4. 469)
22 Nov 1896 S [To teach the monistic ideal to everyone] appears now a very big and a very great undertaking [he said in "The Freedom of the Soul"] (ND 4. 469)
23 Nov 1896 S From first to last, Swamiji taught man's divinity, which stood clear before his eyes. Even when explaining in "Practical Vedanta-IV". (ND 4. 470)
24 Nov 1896 S "The Swami's morning lectures are magnificent," Goodwin wrote to Josephine MacLeod on November 14, apropos of the Tuesday-Thursday classes on practical Vedanta. (ND 4. 472)
25 Nov 1896 S One can but remember and repeat here the words of Mahendra Nath Datta telling of Swamiji's classes earlier that year. (ND 4. 473)
