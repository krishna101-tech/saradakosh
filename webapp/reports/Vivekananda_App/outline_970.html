Detroit (Mar - I 1896)

1 Mar 1896 CE Swami Vivekananda completed his second season of teaching in New York City in the last week of February, 1896. (ND 4.001)
2 Mar 1896 CE Ever since Swamiji's return from England in the early part of December of 1895, he had been planning to visit Detroit and to hold classes in that "dynamic city" of the Midwest. (ND 4.001)
3 Mar 1896 CE It so happened, however, that Swami Kripananda had arrived in Detroit three and a half days in advance of his guru. Only at the last minute had Kripananda decided to go to Detroit. (ND 4.003)

5 Mar 1896 CE It does not seem probable that Swamiji would have requested Kripananda to announce his arrival in Detroit to the local newspapers. (ND 4.004)
6 1 Mar 1896 News reports Kripananda called at the principal newspapers to have the announcement of the Swami's arrival published in the News papers and also started giving interviews to press. (ND 4.005)
7 1 Mar 1896 News reports On Sunday afternoon, Kripananda had yet another interview with a representative from the Free Press, to whom Miss Hamilton may have introduced him. (ND 4.007)
8 2 Mar 1896 News reports In the meanwhile, the Evening News had not been idle. Sniffing a good wrangle in the wind, that journal, always hostile to Swamiji, lost no time in calling on the Right Reverend J. M. Thoburn, a Christian missionary whose contempt For all things Indian could be counted on. (ND 4.008)

31 Mar 1896 Notes Detroit's mixed reaction to Swamiji provides a clear picture of his position vis-a-vis the American religious scene at the end of the nineteenth century. (ND 4.023)
32 15 Mar 1896 Notes Sometime during the second week in March Rabbi met Swamiji to clare up his own doubts, and as a result, the Rabbi offered his temple-the Temple Beth-El-for Swamiji's use on Sunday evening, March 15. (ND 4.028)
33 15 Mar 1896 Notes The newspapers duly announced that Swamiji's lecture would commence at eight o'clock the following evening and would be free. The lecture would be at Templ Beth-El. The News Tribune published a long editorial clearly intended to dissuade all sensible people from attending the lecture. (ND 4.038)
34 15 Mar 1896 Notes The people of Detroit were sorely deranged: they flocked to the Temple Beth-El that evening. (ND 4.039)
35 15 Mar 1896 Notes The lecture was a stunning success. Two or three months later, when they were in London, Mr. Goodwin, recalling these American days, exclaimed to Swamiji, "Our biggest meeting was in Detroit. Nearly 6,000 people! That day your words came out with superhuman strength. I was mad with joy! Six thousand may have been an exaggeration, but even the Detroit newspapers had to admit that the temple had been packed solid. (ND 4.040)
36 Mar 1896 Notes Swamiji had many friends in Detroit. (ND 4.029)
37 Mar 1896 Notes Swamiji had less time than he had had in 1894 to accept formal invitations. Yet no doubt he often dined informally at the homes of his close friends, and no one could be as delightfully informal as Swamiji. (ND 4.029)
38 Mar 1896 Notes On March 11 Mr. Goodwin wrote to Mrs. Bull, informing her of Kripananda's distressing and, one might say, distraught accusations. (ND 4.030)
39 Mar 1896 Notes There is no way of knowing how many disciples Swamiji made in Detroit, but that many people became his followers is certain, and to be a direct follower of his was no small matter. (ND 4.030)
40 Mar 1896 Notes After months of smoldering, the fire of Kripananda's jealousy seems to have totally destroyed his judgment; one can only think that a sort of madness possessed him. (ND 4.032)
41 Mar 1896 Notes Kripananda's stars seem to have been particularly awry during that month of March 1896. (ND 4.034)
42 Mar 1896 Notes Swamiji received Mr. Flagg's letter after he left Detroit and turned it over to Mrs. Bull to act upon. She promptly copied it out sad, for his information, sent it on to Kripananda. (ND 4.035)
