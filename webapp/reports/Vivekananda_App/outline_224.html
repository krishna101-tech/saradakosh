Southern California: Pasadena (Jan - Feb 1900)

1 24 Dec 1899 S Swamiji and Miss MacLeod first visited the Mead sisters on December 24. (ND 5. 242)
2 Dec 1899 S Swamiji went for dinner at the home of Mead sisters, Pasadena and agreed to come and stay with them. (ND 5. 243)
3 Dec 1899 S In 1900 the town of Pasadena, then barely twenty-five years old, covered six square miles and was inhabited by 1o,ooo pleased people, most of whom had come from the Midwest and East. (ND 5. 265)
4 Dec 1899 S Swamiji was also a guest of others at Pasadena during this period. (ND 5. 243 LSV 2. 498)
5 13 Jan 1900 CE Swamiji was persuaded during his stay in Pasadena to make excursions here and there. (ND 5.244 LSV 2.502)
6 Jan 1900 CE It was not until the second weekend in January that Swamiji made the trip up Mount Lowe, a high peak of the San Gabriel Range that rose just northeast of Pasadena. (ND 5.244)
7 Jan 1900 CE From Mount Lowe entire party went to Echo Summit. (ND 5.245)
8 13 Jan 1900 CE Swamiji, Mr. and Mrs. Baumgardt, Miss MacLeod, and Mrs. Leggett looked at the stars through the telescope at the nearby Lowe Observatory: (ND 5.245)
9 14 Jan 1900 CE In the late morning or early afternoon of Sunday, January 14, they boarded a small trolley car and proceeded up the mountain. Just three weeks after his stay at Echo Mountain House, that edifice, with all its seventy bedrooms, burned to the ground. (ND 5.245)
10 14 Jan 1900 News reports News Report in the Los Angeles Times. (ND 5. 266)
11 14 Jan 1900 S The Swami's work in Pasadena started with a lecture in the small theatre of the then famous and luxurious Green Hotel on the morning of Monday, January 15. (ND 5.266 LSV 2. 499)
12 Jan 1900 News reports The Pasadena Star, also announced dates of further lectures of Swamiji. (ND 5.266)
13 Jan 1900 S The Green was as elegant a hotel as one could find anywhere in that age of elegant hotels. (ND 5.267)
14 Jan 1900 S Admission to Swamiji’s three morning lectures, or classes, at the Green was free. (ND 5.268)
15 15 Jan 1900 CE The largest number of the Swami's Pasadena lectures were delivered at the Shakespeare Club. (LSV 2. 499)
16 16 Jan 1900 CE Swamiji’s excursion up Mount Lowe was followed almost immediately by a trip to Redlands, a small town some seventy miles east of Los Angeles. (ND 5. 246)
17 16 Jan 1900 News reports The Daily Facts of January i6 printed the following item: (ND 5. 247)
18 16 Jan 1900 News reports It is certain that Swamiji did not stay overnight at Redlands and most probable that he did not speak there, for on the evening of January 16 he had a lecture engagement at the Shakespeare Club in Pasadena. (ND 5. 247)
19 17 Jan 1900 News reports News paper Report of Los Angels Times of January 17. (ND 5. 268)
20 18 Jan 1900 S Swamiji's second lecture was on January 18, He asked audience to suggest the subject. The subject decided was " The Indian Women". (ND 5. 269)
21 18 Jan 1900 S Swamiji gave, extemporaneously as always, one of his most beautiful and comprehensive talks on Hindu women. (ND 5. 270)
22 18 Jan 1900 S Swamiji did not spare critics in the audience. (ND 5. 270)
23 18 Jan 1900 S Certain members in the audience made an attempt to trap Swamiji into saying something that would discredit him. (ND 5. 271)
24 18 Jan 1900 I One of the women in particular set out to corner Swamiji. This unpleasant incident constituted Swamiji’s only direct encounter with missionary groups during his second visit to the West. (ND 5. 271)
25 Jan 1900 CE The Shakespeare Club, where this collision took place and where Swamiji was to speak at least twelve more times during the following two weeks, was the queen of the town’s women’s clubs. (ND 5. 272)
26 21 Jan 1900 News reports Los Angeles Times reported about the receptions given to Swmiji at the Shakespeare Club snd at Hotel Green. (ND 5. 274)
27 21 Jan 1900 News reports The Los Angeles Herald of January 21 also commented on the reception: (ND 5. 274)

29 21 Jan 1900 S The announcement in the above newspaper items that Swami Vivekananda was to speak almost every morning of that week. (ND 5. 275)

34 Jan 1900 I A memorable incedent occurred on one Sunday. To Mrs. Hansbrough the moment was clearly a sacred one; it seemed that her daughter and nephew had received a special blessing, to which she gave the name "baptism." (ND 5. 253)
35 Jan 1900 I one more incident at Mead's house (ND 5. 254)
36 Jan 1900 CE Swamiji held a ten o’clock class in nearby Pasadena. On such occasions he would leave the house around nine or nine-thirty, accompanied by Mrs. Hansbrough. (ND 5. 254)
37 Jan 1900 CE Swamiji was taking treatment from Mrs. Melton and was feeling well. (ND 5. 255)
38 Jan 1900 CE Not only did Swamiji come to all meals, but he ate almost every kind of food, apparently without ill effect. (ND 5. 255)
39 Jan 1900 S Often Swamiji would invite one person or another from his morning class to lunch at the Meads (ND 5. 256)
40 Jan 1900 S But the best lunches of all were the picnics that took place on the open top of the nearby hill. (ND 5. 256 LSV 2. 502)
41 Jan 1900 S Mrs. Wyckoff once experienced relief from physical and mental suffering just through contact with an object that had belonged to Swamiji-his pipe. (ND 5. 258)
42 Jan 1900 S It was this steady miracle of grace, that made the Mead sisters feel, from moment to moment, that Christ himself was there. (ND 5. 259)
43 Jan 1900 S Swamiji's routine was simple. (ND 5. 259)
44 Jan 1900 S Perhaps Swamiji also did some writing in the afternoons. (ND 5. 260)
45 Jan 1900 S Swamiji would sometimes give private interviews in the afternoons or talk with visitors who came to see the Mead sisters. (ND 5. 261)
46 Jan 1900 S In the late afternoons Swamiji would help Mrs. Wyckoff prepare dinner, and often he himself cooked the whole meal. (ND 5. 261)
47 Jan 1900 S Among those who joined this family gathering was Miss MacLeod, who for a few days was a house guest of the Meads’, sleeping, as we have seen, in the already crowded front bedroom. (ND 5. 263)
48 Jan 1900 S Mead sisters may not, strictly speaking, have been Swamiji’s disciples, they loved and served him devotedly. (ND 5. 264)
49 Jan 1900 CE Mrs. Hansbrough was, indeed, to act as his secretary and, for a time, his housekeeper during his coming stay in northern California. (LSV 2. 504)
