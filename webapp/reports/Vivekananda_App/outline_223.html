3 7 Nov 1899 S Swamiji departed Ridgley and arrived at NY. In the evening he went to the new Office and Library of the Vedant Society, which he had founded five years earlier. (ND 5.145 LSV 2.485)
4 Nov 1899 S Peramanent quarters of Vedanta society were formally opened by Swami Abhedananda at 146 east 55th street. (ND 5.145 LSV 2.485)
5 7 Nov 1899 S Swamiji was introduced by Swami Abhedananda in a regular weekly lecture at Vedant Society. He precided and gave the evening to questions and answers. (ND 5.146)
6 Nov 1899 S Swami Abhedananda walked down with Swamiji to Mr. Leggett’s house [at 21 West Thirty-fourth Street]." (ND 5.146)
7 8 Nov 1899 S Next day Swami Abhadananda went with S.V. to Dr. Guerandzy [Guernsey] and lunched with him. The Guernseys looked upon Swamiji as a son, and he was ever welcome in their home. All about Dr. Guernseys. (ND 5.147)
8 9 Nov 1899 S Swamiji’s stay at Ridgely Manor had restored his health to some extent, but, as Gurudasa noted, he was still far from well. he moved from the Leggetts’ house to the Guernseys’. (ND 5.159)
9 Nov 1899 I Swamiji suffered an emotional shock at the unexpected sight of one (Mr. Leon Landsberg) who, lamentably, had closed his heart. Along with Swamiji's letter to Miss MacLeod (which in the Complete Works has been wrongly dated "June, 1895"). (ND 5.148)
10 Nov 1899 S Swamiji had last seen his disciple Leon Landsberg in March of 1896. Since that time Landsberg had not fared well; his story was, indeed, a dismal one. A brief review. (ND 5.148)
11 Nov 1899 S Mrs. Bull, through many letters, persuaded Landsberg to return at least in spirit. (ND 5.150)
12 Nov 1899 S We now lose all track of Kripananda until Sunday, March 27, 1898. On that day there appeared in the, New York Herald a full page illustrated article. A large banner headline and prominent sub-headings read: (ND 5.152)
13 10 Nov 1899 ME In the evening, the New York Vedanta Society held a reception for Swamiji. (ND 5.155 LSV 2.486)
14 10 Nov 1899 I Swamiji seldom willed to use his power and never wished to impose his ideas upon the minds of others, as he so easily could. An incident took place at one of Swamiji’s class talks or lectures during this period in New York that, aware of his power, he suddenly stopped and left the room. (ND 5.157)
